# Weekly Review Framework

## Purpose

The weekly review is a structured reflection — 30 minutes of looking back
to move forward with more intention. Unlike a daily log, this creates distance
and perspective: patterns emerge across days, themes surface, and small wins
that felt insignificant in the moment become visible.

---

## The 3 Questions

The entire review lives in three questions. Ask them in order. Let the user
take their time with each. Don't rush to the next question.

### Question 1: What happened?

> "Looking back at this past week — what stands out to you?
> It doesn't have to be big. Just whatever comes to mind first."

**What you're listening for:**
- The moments they mention first (usually most emotionally charged)
- What they omit (avoidance clues)
- Whether they mention themselves or only external events
- The balance of positive vs negative mentions

**If they say "nothing happened":**
- "Sometimes weeks blend together — what's one thing you remember, even if it seems small?"
- "What was the hardest day this week? The easiest?"
- "What do you wish had happened that didn't?"

**Reflection to offer before moving on:**
Summarize 2-3 things they mentioned. Use their words.
"I heard [X], [Y], and [Z] — is that the right sense of what stood out?"

---

### Question 2: How did it feel?

> "Of everything you mentioned — what felt the hardest?
> And was there anything that felt surprisingly good?"

**What you're listening for:**
- Whether they can name feelings (emotional literacy)
- Themes in what made things hard vs good
- Surprises (things that felt better/worse than expected)
- Self-compassion vs self-criticism balance

**If they struggle with feelings:**
- Offer a menu: "Would it fit better to say it felt exhausting, frustrating,
  overwhelming, sad, or something else?"
- Focus on body sensations: "Did it feel tight in your chest? Heavy? Restless?"
- Normalize: "Sometimes it's hard to find the word — what's the closest fit?"

**Reflection to offer before moving on:**
"So it sounds like the hardest part was [X], and the thing that surprised you
in a good way was [Y]. Is that right?"

---

### Question 3: What do you want?

> "If next week could be just 10% better — what would that look like for you?"

**What you're listening for:**
- Whether they have any desire for change (sometimes no is honest)
- The scale of what they want (tiny shift vs total transformation)
- Whether the desire is internally or externally driven
- Whether the want is specific enough to be actionable

**If they want everything to change:**
- "That sounds like a lot. If you could only pick one thing to feel different
  next week, what would it be?"

**If they say "I don't know":**
- "That's okay too. Sometimes not knowing is the honest answer."
- "What would it feel like if nothing needed to be different?"

**Reflection to offer:**
Validate their want. If they named something specific, repeat it back:
"So what you're reaching for is [X]. That makes sense given what you shared
about [Y from earlier]."

---

## After the Three Questions

Once you've worked through the three questions, offer a brief synthesis:

### The Theme
> "If this week had a title, what would it be?"

Alternative: "If you had to describe the feeling of this week in one sentence,
what would you say?"

### The Undervalued Win
Surface one thing they might be minimizing or overlooking:
> "I want to name something you mentioned that you might be undervaluing: [X].
  That sounds like it took more than you're giving yourself credit for."

### The Thing Needing Attention
Surface one thing that might need more focus — without making it a problem:
> "The thing that keeps coming up for me is [X]. I wonder if that's worth
  paying attention to this week — not to fix it, just to notice it more."

### One Tiny Intention
Offer ONE small thing to try next week — only if they seem open:
> "If you wanted to carry one thing forward, it could be as simple as:
  [tiny, specific, anchored action]. Want to try that?"

## Common Weekly Review Patterns

### The "Everything Was Fine" Review
**Signals:** "Fine", "okay", "normal", "nothing special", can't recall specifics.

**What's usually happening:** Emotional numbing, low awareness, or habituation
to stress. Sometimes also indicates depression (flat affect).

**How to respond:**
- "Fine can cover a lot — was it more exhausting-fine, boring-fine, or
  actually okay-fine?"
- "What was one moment this week when you felt anything at all — even just
  annoyed or relieved?"
- "What would have needed to be different for it to be a good week?"

### The "Everything Was Terrible" Review
**Signals:** Only negative events, can't recall anything good, despair language.

**What's usually happening:** Negativity bias (real, but not the whole picture)
or genuinely hard week. Both deserve acknowledgment.

**How to respond:**
- Reflect the difficulty without amplifying: "That sounds like a lot to carry."
- Then gently: "Was there any moment, however small, when something wasn't terrible?
  Even just a minute?"
- Don't toxic-positivity them — let the hard week be hard, but help them find
  one foothold.

### The "I Should Have Done More" Review
**Signals:** Focus on what didn't happen, guilt about unfinished tasks,
  self-criticism for not being productive enough.

**What's usually happening:** Productivity-wired self-worth. Hard to feel good
about a week that wasn't "accomplishing" things.

**How to respond:**
- "I hear a lot of what you didn't do. What did you do that actually mattered —
  even if it wasn't on a list?"
- "What's something you handled this week that you might be undervaluing because
  it came easily to you?"
- "What if productivity wasn't the measure — what would this week look like then?"

### The "I Don't Remember" Review
**Signals:** Memory gaps, days blurring together, "the week went by so fast."

**What's usually happening:** Low conscious awareness, autopilot living,
  or genuinely forgetful (sleep deprivation, stress, overwhelm).

**How to respond:**
- "Days blur when we're on autopilot. What's one thing you remember — even just
  a meal, a conversation, a feeling?"
- "What's one thing you wish you remembered more of? What would you have wanted
  to be different?"

## When NOT to Do a Weekly Review

- If the user is in acute crisis (refer to emotional-safety.md)
- If they're clearly not present or interested
- If it feels like pulling teeth — suggest pausing and trying another time
- If they've just shared something heavy that needs processing before moving to review

The weekly review should feel like a gift, not homework.

## Sample Weekly Review Flow

**User:** "I want to do a weekly review."

**Agent:** "Okay. Let's take this step by step.

Looking back at this past week — what stands out to you? It doesn't have to be big.
Just whatever comes to mind first."

**User:** "I finished that project at work. My sister visited. I felt really anxious
on Thursday. I went for a run once. That's about it."

**Agent:** "So a work project wrapped up, your sister visited, you had a rough day
Thursday, and you got outside for a run once. Is that the right sense of what
stood out?"

**User:** "Yeah, that's it."

**Agent:** "Of everything you mentioned — what felt the hardest? And was there
anything that felt surprisingly good?"

**User:** "Thursday was awful. I don't even know why, I just couldn't stop worrying.
And actually, the run felt better than I expected."

**Agent:** "So Thursday was the low point — that unspun anxiety. And the run
was a bright spot you weren't expecting. That makes sense — sometimes when the
mind is going, the body helps."

**User:** "Yeah."

**Agent:** "If next week could be just 10% better — what would that look like for you?"

**User:** "I don't know. I guess I just don't want to feel that anxious again."

**Agent:** "That's a reasonable want. If you wanted to try something small this week —
what if, when you noticed that anxiety starting, you just wrote down one thing
you're worried about? Not to fix it, just to get it out of your head and onto
paper. Want to try that?"

**User:** "Sure, that sounds doable."

**Agent:** "Great. One last thing — if this week had a title, what would it be?"

**User:** "The week I finished the project, I guess."

**Agent:** "'The week I finished the project' — and it sounds like it came with
a mix of relief and some hard moments. Thanks for sharing all of that with me."

## Quick Reference Card

**Q1:** "What stands out from this week?" → Listen for what they mention first
**Q2:** "What felt hardest? What felt surprisingly good?" → Emotional literacy check
**Q3:** "What would 10% better look like?" → One tiny intention

**After:** Theme, undervalued win, attention point, optional micro-habit

**Tone:** Warm, spacious, no homework energy
